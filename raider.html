The GPU in New Budget Gaming PCs

The most-sold “budget” GPU is a laptop chip in a costume.

AMD designed the Navi 24 silicon — the chip inside the RX 6500 XT and the RX 6400 that ships in Victus desktops — as a mobile GPU for laptops.  When the 2022 chip shortage crushed desktop GPU availability, AMD rushed it into a desktop card to fill the gap.  It shipped with every compromise a laptop GPU has to make, and the reviews were brutal.  Four years later, OEMs still ship it because it fits their margin spreadsheet — not because it should be in your machine.

Here’s what you’re actually avoiding by picking refurbished.

The Bus Width
PCIe 4.0 x4 only

A normal desktop GPU uses a PCIe x16 slot.  This one uses only 4 lanes — a quarter of the bandwidth.  On the PCIe 3.0 motherboards still common in budget PCs, it gets cut again to one-eighth of normal.

Translation: the game stutters when it runs out of VRAM.  Which is constantly.

The Bandwidth Trap
4 GB — fed by a straw

Your Raider has the same 4 GB of VRAM as a new RX 6400.  The difference is what’s feeding it: a full 16-lane PCIe bus versus a crippled 4-lane bus.  Same capacity, four times the bandwidth — textures load faster, transitions don’t stutter, the GPU never sits starved waiting on data.

Old 4 GB + fast bus beats new 4 GB + slow bus.  Every benchmark.  Every time.

The Missing Encoder
No video streaming

AMD removed the hardware H.264 and H.265 encoders from this chip to save silicon.  You cannot stream to Twitch, record gameplay, or run a smooth video call on this card.  Every other GPU on Earth has this.

Even free iGPUs from Intel have hardware encoders.  This one doesn’t.

The Missing Outputs
Only 2 display ports

One HDMI, one DisplayPort.  That’s it.  A normal budget GPU has three or four.  Want to run a second monitor for Discord?  Or plug in a TV on the side?  Not on this card.

This is a laptop-grade design decision shipped in a desktop case.

A 2016 RX 470 from the Raider era has the same 4 GB of VRAM as a new RX 6400 — on a PCIe bus that moves data four times faster, with hardware H.264 and H.265 encoders the new card doesn’t have, and three display outputs instead of two.  Older hardware beats newer hardware on every axis that matters — because the new one was built to a price, not a purpose.

Same Memory.  Different Outcome.

The 16 GB Victus will still stutter.

Once we equalize the RAM, the Victus should run smoothly — right?  It won’t.  The stutter just moves.  In the 8 GB config, the bottleneck was system memory — Windows 11 paging to the SSD every time Fortnite wanted more than 1.5 GB.  In the 16 GB config, system memory is fine.  The GPU becomes the bottleneck instead.

The RX 6400 has 4 GB of VRAM — the same as the RX 470 in your Raider.  Modern games routinely request 5–6 GB of textures at 1080p Medium.  When VRAM fills up, the GPU reaches over the PCIe bus to pull textures from system RAM.  On a normal GPU (like yours), that bus is 16 lanes wide.  On the RX 6400, it’s 4 lanes wide.  The GPU sits starved, waiting for data to arrive one quarter as fast as it should.

That’s what you feel as stutter.  Not the RAM paging.  The GPU starving.  Add more system RAM and you don’t fix it — because the pipe between system RAM and GPU is exactly as narrow as it was at 8 GB.

When the Victus GPU runs out of VRAM — which happens on the third map load in any modern game — it reaches through a bus that’s four times narrower than yours.  That’s the stutter.  Adding RAM doesn’t widen the pipe.  Only a real GPU on a real bus does.

Our RR Turbo 11 Pro still delivers a working-memory advantage even at matched RAM — Windows 11 Home stock consumes ~6.5 GB at idle, while RR Turbo runs at ~3.2 GB.  That’s 3–4 GB of free headroom you paid for and get to actually use.  But the real story is the GPU bus, not the system RAM.  Fix one, the other still breaks the game.
